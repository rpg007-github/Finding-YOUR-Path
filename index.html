Welcome. Let's Talk.

If you are reading this, you are probably thinking about a career in healthcare or biomedical science. Maybe you have known since you were twelve that you wanted to be a doctor. Maybe you just realized it last semester. Maybe you are not sure at all and someone told you that you "should go to med school" because you are smart. Whatever brought you here, you are in the right place.

This site was built by a faculty member who has spent decades advising students just like you. The goal is simple: give you honest, clear information so you can make the best decision for your life. Not your parents' dream. Not what looks impressive on paper. Yours.

"A wise person changes their mind. A fool never does." -- a truth that applies to career paths, too

Here is the most important thing this site will tell you: it is completely OK to change your mind. You do not have to be a physician to have a meaningful, fulfilling career. There are many ways to help people, contribute to science, earn a good living, and feel proud of what you do every day. If at any point while reading this you think "maybe this is not for me," that is not failure. That is wisdom. That is self-awareness. That is a strength.

But if you DO want it? Then commit. Make a plan. Find mentors. Put in the work. Passion without action is just daydreaming. As a wise character once said: "Do or do not. There is no try." If you find yourself constantly looking for reasons why it will not work, rather than pushing yourself to make it work, then pause and be honest about what you truly want. And that honesty, either way, is the first step toward a great life.

Key Terms You Need to Know

Before we go any further, let's make sure we speak the same language. These terms come up constantly, and understanding them now will save you confusion later.

MD (Doctor of Medicine)
The degree awarded by allopathic medical schools. "Allopathic" simply means conventional Western medicine. MDs can practice in any medical specialty, prescribe medications, and perform surgery. There are approximately 158 MD-granting medical schools in the U.S., accredited by the LCME (Liaison Committee on Medical Education).
DO (Doctor of Osteopathic Medicine)
The degree awarded by osteopathic medical schools. DOs receive the same training as MDs and can do everything an MD can do: diagnose, prescribe, perform surgery, and specialize. The key difference is that osteopathic training includes additional coursework in osteopathic manipulative treatment (OMT), a hands-on approach to musculoskeletal care. DOs and MDs are licensed identically. There are approximately 38 colleges of osteopathic medicine (with 60+ campuses) in the U.S., accredited by the COCA (Commission on Osteopathic College Accreditation). The application service is called AACOMAS.
PA (Physician Assistant / Physician Associate)
A licensed healthcare provider who practices medicine on a team led by or in collaboration with a physician. PAs can examine patients, diagnose conditions, prescribe medications, and assist in surgery. PA programs typically require a bachelor's degree plus clinical experience before admission, and the PA program itself is a master's degree taking about 27 months. PAs apply through CASPA (Centralized Application Service for Physician Assistants). The certifying exam is called the PANCE.
Biomedical Graduate School (M.S. / Ph.D.)
Graduate programs in biomedical fields such as biochemistry, pharmacology, neuroscience, molecular biology, microbiology, immunology, public health, or biomedical engineering. A Master of Science (M.S.) typically takes 2 years. A Doctor of Philosophy (Ph.D.) typically takes 4 to 7 years and focuses on original research, culminating in a dissertation. Many Ph.D. programs in biomedical sciences offer full tuition waivers and a stipend.
GPA (Grade Point Average)
A numerical measure of your academic performance, typically on a 4.0 scale. Medical schools look at your cumulative GPA (all courses), science GPA or BCPM (Biology, Chemistry, Physics, Math courses only), and sometimes your "trend" (whether your grades improved over time).
MCAT (Medical College Admission Test)
A standardized exam required by almost all MD and DO schools. It covers four sections: Chemical and Physical Foundations of Biological Systems, Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills (CARS), Biological and Biochemical Foundations of Living Systems, and Psychological, Social, and Biological Foundations of Behavior. Scores range from 472 to 528, with a median of 500. Most competitive applicants score 508 or higher for MD schools and 500 or higher for DO schools.
GRE (Graduate Record Examination)
A standardized test sometimes required for PA programs (though increasingly optional) and often required for biomedical graduate school. It tests verbal reasoning, quantitative reasoning, and analytical writing.
AMCAS / AACOMAS / CASPA
These are centralized application services. AMCAS (American Medical College Application Service) is for MD schools. AACOMAS (American Association of Colleges of Osteopathic Medicine Application Service) is for DO schools. CASPA (Centralized Application Service for Physician Assistants) is for PA programs. Each allows you to submit one primary application to multiple schools.
Clinical Hours / Direct Patient Care Experience (PCE)
Hands-on time spent working directly with patients. This is measured in hours and is particularly critical for PA school applications. Activities that count include working as an EMT, medical assistant, CNA, phlebotomist, paramedic, or scribe (some programs). Shadowing a physician typically counts separately as "observation hours" rather than direct patient care.
MSAR (Medical School Admission Requirements)
An online database maintained by the AAMC that contains detailed admissions data for every accredited MD school: average GPA, MCAT scores, acceptance rates, tuition, and specific prerequisites. It costs about $28/year and is considered an essential tool for any serious applicant. The DO equivalent is the Choose DO Explorer on AACOM's website.
Holistic Review
An admissions approach where the committee evaluates the whole applicant, not just numbers. This includes experiences, personal qualities, leadership, community service, research, overcoming adversity, and demographic background. Most medical schools now describe their process as "holistic," but that does not mean numbers do not matter. It means numbers alone do not tell your whole story.
Primary Care Track vs. Physician-Scientist Track
Some medical schools emphasize training doctors who will see patients in underserved communities (primary care focus). Others emphasize training physician-scientists who will conduct research alongside clinical work, often through MD/Ph.D. programs. Understanding a school's mission helps you find the best fit. A school focused on producing rural family doctors will evaluate your application differently than one focused on producing NIH-funded researchers.

Understanding Your Options

One of the biggest mistakes students make is treating "medical school" as the only option for a career in healthcare or biomedical science. It is not. There are multiple paths, and each one has distinct advantages depending on what you value most: patient interaction, research, lifestyle, autonomy, length of training, or scope of practice. Let's break them down honestly.

Why Choose MD?

You want full autonomy in clinical practice. You want access to every specialty, from dermatology to neurosurgery. You are drawn to the idea of being the person who makes the final call on patient care. You may also be interested in academic medicine, where you combine research and teaching with clinical practice. You are willing to commit to a long training pipeline (a minimum of 7 years after college, often more), and you accept the significant financial investment. MD training opens the widest range of doors in clinical medicine, and the degree carries universal recognition worldwide.

Why Choose DO?

You want to be a fully licensed physician with all the same rights and capabilities as an MD, but you are also attracted to the osteopathic philosophy: treat the whole person, emphasize preventive care, and understand the musculoskeletal system deeply. DO schools also tend to have slightly lower average GPA and MCAT thresholds for admission, which does not mean they are easier, it means they may evaluate your application differently and weight clinical experience or personal story more heavily. If you want to be a primary care physician or are drawn to community-based medicine, DO programs are an excellent fit. Importantly, DOs and MDs now enter the same residency match system (since 2020), so the historical distinction in residency access has largely disappeared.

Why Choose PA?

You want to practice medicine, see patients, diagnose, and treat, but you want a shorter training pipeline and more flexibility. PAs can switch specialties without additional residency training. If you discover you love orthopedics after working in family medicine for five years, you can make that switch. The PA profession offers a strong salary (national median around $130K), excellent job growth, and a lifestyle that many find more sustainable than physician training. You work collaboratively as part of a medical team. If you love patient care but do not need full independent practice authority, PA may be the smartest path for you.

Why Choose Biomedical Graduate School?

You are fascinated by questions more than answers. You love being in the lab, designing experiments, analyzing data, and pushing the boundaries of what we know about biology and disease. You want to discover the next drug target, develop a diagnostic tool, understand how a disease works at the molecular level, or teach the next generation of scientists. A Ph.D. is the path for you if research, not patient care, is your primary drive. It is also worth noting that Ph.D. programs in the biomedical sciences are usually fully funded, meaning you get paid a stipend and do not pay tuition, which is the opposite of medical school. You can also pursue combined MD/Ph.D. programs if you truly want to do both (these are typically 7 to 8 years and also fully funded). Careers after a biomedical Ph.D. include academic professor, industry scientist, biotech entrepreneur, government researcher (NIH, CDC, FDA), science policy advisor, patent law (with additional training), medical writer, and more.

The Honest Conversation About "Prestige"

Let's address this directly, because it is one of the biggest sources of stress and bad decision-making among pre-health students. Some students (and some parents) view the paths above as a hierarchy: MD at the top, then DO, then PA, then "everything else." This is wrong, and it leads people to pursue paths that do not fit them.

A PA who loves their work, sees patients every day, earns a strong salary, and goes home to their family at a reasonable hour is not "less than" a burned-out physician who chose medicine for the wrong reasons. A Ph.D. scientist whose research leads to a new cancer treatment has contributed something extraordinary to humanity, even though they never wore a white coat in a hospital. A DO in a rural community may be the only doctor for miles, changing and saving lives daily.

The only "right" path is the one that aligns with who you actually are, what genuinely excites you, and how you want to spend your days. Not the one that sounds most impressive at a family dinner.

What This Table Tells You

The overall acceptance rate for MD schools is about 42%. That means the majority of applicants are rejected, even many with strong numbers. Look at the pattern: a student with a 3.80+ GPA and a 510+ MCAT has roughly a two-in-three chance. But a student with a 3.40 GPA and a 500 MCAT has less than a one-in-five chance at MD schools. This is not meant to discourage you. It is meant to help you be strategic.

Key insight: Notice that MCAT scores have enormous impact on acceptance rates within any GPA range. A student with a 3.40 to 3.59 GPA who scores above 517 on the MCAT has a 61% acceptance rate, while the same GPA with a sub-497 MCAT drops to about 5%. MCAT preparation is one of the highest-return investments you can make.

DO School Numbers

The average GPA for DO school matriculants is approximately 3.62 overall and 3.55 for science courses. The average MCAT is approximately 504 to 505. These are real, achievable numbers. DO schools tend to place heavier emphasis on clinical experience, community service, and fit with the osteopathic philosophy. If your GPA is in the 3.4 to 3.6 range and your MCAT is around 500 to 508, DO schools should be a strong part of your application strategy.

PA School Numbers

The average accepted PA student has approximately a 3.6 overall GPA and 3.5 science GPA. Most programs require 500 to 2,000+ hours of direct patient care experience, and competitive applicants often have well over 1,000 hours. The GRE is required by fewer than 15% of programs as of 2025. PA school acceptance rates hover around 30% overall, but vary enormously by program.

Graduate School Numbers

Biomedical Ph.D. programs vary widely but generally look for a GPA of 3.0 or above (3.3+ is more competitive), strong research experience (ideally 1 to 2 years in a lab), letters of recommendation from research mentors, and a clear statement of research interests. Many programs have dropped the GRE requirement. Acceptance rates vary by program prestige, from 5% at top-tier programs to 30 to 50% at others.

Understanding How Different Schools Review Applications

Not all medical schools are the same, and understanding their missions is critical for building a smart school list. Here are the major categories:

"Doctors to See Patients" Schools (Primary Care / Community Focus)

These schools emphasize producing physicians who will practice in underserved areas, often in primary care (family medicine, internal medicine, pediatrics, OB/GYN). They tend to value clinical volunteering, community service, rural or underserved experience, and a personal narrative about wanting to serve. Examples include many state-funded medical schools and schools with explicit rural training tracks. In your application, they want to see that you have actually spent time in the communities you say you want to serve.

"Physician-Scientist" Schools (Research Focus)

These schools (often at major research universities) emphasize producing physicians who will also conduct biomedical research. They value strong research experience, publications, presentations, and evidence of scholarly curiosity. They may offer MD/Ph.D. programs. In your application, they want to see research productivity, a clear understanding of the scientific method, and the ability to ask good questions. Think institutions like NIH-funded academic medical centers.

"Balanced" Schools

Most schools fall somewhere in between, training physicians for a broad range of careers. They look for well-rounded applicants with both clinical and academic/research experiences. The majority of medical schools fall into this category.

Strategy tip: When building your school list, read each school's mission statement carefully. If a school says "we train physicians to serve rural communities" and you have zero rural experience, that is probably not a great fit, regardless of your numbers. Conversely, if a school emphasizes research and you have two years of productive lab work, that is a signal to apply even if your GPA is slightly below their average.

Preparation is a Marathon, Not a Sprint

The students who succeed are not necessarily the ones with the most natural talent. They are the ones who made a plan early, stayed consistent, and asked for help when they needed it. Below is a stage-by-stage guide, whether you are starting in high school or are already deep into college.

If You Are in High School

Freshman & Sophomore Year
Focus on building strong study habits and a genuine love of science. Take the hardest science and math courses your school offers (AP Biology, AP Chemistry, AP Physics, honors math). Earn the best grades you can, but also explore activities that show who you are as a person: volunteer at a hospital or nursing home, join clubs, develop leadership skills, read broadly. You do not need to have your entire career figured out. You need to be curious and disciplined.
Junior Year
Start researching colleges that have strong pre-health advising, good science departments, and access to clinical volunteer opportunities. Look for schools where you can get to know your professors personally (this matters enormously for letters of recommendation later). Consider whether a large research university or a smaller college suits your learning style better. Take the SAT or ACT seriously because getting into a good-fit college is step one. If possible, shadow a physician, PA, or researcher over the summer to start understanding what these careers actually look like day to day.
Senior Year & Summer Before College
Apply to colleges. Once accepted, start planning your first-year course schedule with pre-health requirements in mind (typically general chemistry and biology). Over the summer before college, get clinical exposure if you can: volunteer at a hospital, shadow a healthcare professional, or work as a CNA. Every hour counts and, more importantly, it helps you confirm whether you actually like being around patients and medical settings.

If You Are in College

Freshman Year
Take General Biology I and II and General Chemistry I and II if your background supports it. Focus on learning how to study at the college level; this is harder than high school and most students need to upgrade their approach. Visit office hours regularly. Start volunteering in a clinical setting, even if it is just a few hours a week. Join one or two organizations related to your interests (pre-med club, research club, community service). Do NOT overload yourself. A 3.9 GPA with modest extracurriculars is far better than a 3.3 GPA with a packed resume.
Sophomore Year
Take Organic Chemistry I and II, and continue with biology electives (anatomy, physiology, genetics, microbiology). Start looking for a research lab if you are interested in MD or Ph.D. paths. Approach a professor whose work interests you and ask if you can join their lab, even as a volunteer initially. Continue accumulating clinical hours. If you are considering the PA path, start logging direct patient care hours seriously (get a job as an EMT, CNA, medical assistant, or scribe). This is also the time to build relationships with 2 to 3 professors who can write you strong, personal letters of recommendation.
Junior Year
Complete Biochemistry, Physics I and II, and any remaining prerequisites. If you are applying to medical school, plan your MCAT timeline: most students take it in the spring of junior year or summer before senior year. Begin MCAT preparation 3 to 6 months before your test date. This is also the year to deepen your involvements rather than add new ones. Admissions committees value sustained commitment over a scattered list of one-time activities. Talk to your pre-health advisor about your school list, timeline, and any concerns about your application.
Senior Year (or Growth Year)
Applications open in May/June for the following year's entering class. AMCAS, AACOMAS, and CASPA all have their own timelines; apply early because most schools use rolling admissions. Many successful applicants take one or more growth years to strengthen their applications with additional clinical experience, research, MCAT improvement, or personal development. A growth year is not a gap. It is an investment. About 65% of medical school matriculants did not go straight from college to medical school. They used that time to grow, and they were better for it.

It Is a "Growth Year," Not a "Gap Year"

Language matters, especially the language you use about yourself. The phrase "gap year" implies a hole, a pause, something missing. But there is nothing missing about a year spent gaining 1,500 hours of clinical experience, improving your MCAT by 8 points, publishing a research paper, or simply maturing into someone who is genuinely ready for the intensity of professional school. That is not a gap. That is growth.

About 65% of students who matriculate into medical school took at least one year between finishing college and starting medical school. Many took two or three. Some of the strongest, most focused students in any entering class are the ones who did not rush. They used that time to become better applicants and, more importantly, better future clinicians and scientists.

Reframe it in your mind and in your conversations: "I am taking a growth year to strengthen my clinical experience and prepare for the MCAT" sounds different than "I am taking a gap year because I was not ready." They might describe the same situation, but one frames you as strategic and intentional, which is exactly what you are being.

What does a productive growth year look like? It looks different for everyone, but the common thread is intentionality. You should have a plan. Maybe you work as a medical assistant or EMT full-time to accumulate patient care hours. Maybe you join a research lab as a post-baccalaureate research assistant. Maybe you take a structured MCAT prep course. Maybe you volunteer with an underserved community to build the experiences that will fuel your personal statement. Maybe you do several of these at once. The point is that you are not "waiting." You are actively building toward your goal.

If someone, a family member, a peer, anyone, makes you feel bad about not going straight through, remember this: medical schools do not penalize growth years. They reward them. An applicant who spent a year as an EMT responding to real emergencies brings something to the table that a 22-year-old who went straight from organic chemistry to AMCAS simply cannot. Both paths are valid. But never apologize for choosing the one that makes you stronger.

Clinical Hours: What Counts and How Much You Need

For PA programs, direct patient care experience (PCE) is non-negotiable. Common roles that qualify include: EMT or paramedic, certified nursing assistant (CNA), medical assistant, phlebotomist, patient care technician, physical therapy aide, and emergency room technician. Scribing is accepted by some programs but not all, so check carefully. Volunteering at a hospital where you transport patients or stock supplies generally does NOT count as direct patient care because you are not performing clinical tasks.

For MD and DO applications, clinical experience is evaluated differently. There is no strict hour requirement, but you need meaningful exposure: physician shadowing (aim for 50 to 100+ hours across multiple specialties), clinical volunteering (consistent, long-term commitment to a hospital, clinic, or free clinic), and ideally some kind of patient-facing role. The key word is "meaningful." Admissions committees want to know that you understand what a physician's life actually looks like and that you chose this path with your eyes open.

For graduate school, clinical hours are generally not required. What matters is research experience: hours in a lab, conference presentations, publications, and a genuine understanding of the research process.

Track your hours from day one. Keep a spreadsheet or log with dates, locations, supervisor names, contact info, and a brief description of what you did. You will need this information for your applications, and it is much easier to record it as you go than to reconstruct it from memory two years later.

The Personal Statement

This is the single most important piece of writing in your application, and most students agonize over it more than they need to. Here is what admissions committees want: they want to understand WHY you want to do this and WHO you are as a person. They do not want a chronological autobiography. They do not want a list of your accomplishments (that is what the rest of the application is for). They want a narrative that reveals something genuine about you.

What Works

Start with a specific moment or experience that crystallized your desire to pursue this path. It does not have to be dramatic. Maybe you watched a PA calmly explain a diagnosis to a frightened elderly patient and thought, "that is what I want to do." Maybe you were in a research lab and had a moment of genuine excitement when an experiment worked after weeks of failure. Whatever it is, make it real and specific. Then connect that moment to the bigger picture: why medicine (or PA, or research), why now, and what kind of practitioner or scientist you want to become. Weave in your experiences naturally as evidence, not as a laundry list.

What Does Not Work

Do not start with "ever since I was five years old, I wanted to be a doctor." Even if it is true, it is a cliche that immediately blends you in with thousands of other applicants. Do not write about how you want to "help people" without explaining specifically what that means to you and why medicine (as opposed to teaching, social work, nursing, or any other helping profession) is your path. Do not name-drop prestigious experiences without reflecting on what they taught you. Do not use the personal statement to explain weaknesses in your application (there are usually other places for that, such as secondary essays or an addendum). And do not try to sound like someone you are not. Authenticity is your most powerful tool.

Start early, revise often. Write a first draft at least 3 to 4 months before your submission deadline. Show it to your pre-health advisor, a trusted professor, and a friend who writes well. Incorporate their feedback. Then revise again. The best personal statements go through 5 to 10 drafts. This is normal.

Letters of Recommendation

Strong letters can make or break your application, and yet most students approach them as an afterthought. Here is how to do it right.

How to Get Great Letters from Professors

Step one is to build a genuine relationship BEFORE you need to ask. Go to office hours, not just when you need help with an assignment, but to discuss the material, ask questions about the professor's research, or talk about your career interests. Do this consistently over at least one semester, ideally two. When a professor knows you as a person, not just a name on a grade sheet, their letter will be specific, enthusiastic, and compelling.

Step two is to ask the right way. When you are ready (typically at least 6 to 8 weeks before your letter is due), approach the professor in person or during office hours. Say something like: "I am applying to [medical school / PA school / graduate school] and I was hoping you might be willing to write me a strong letter of recommendation. I really valued our interactions in [specific class or research setting], and I think you could speak to [specific qualities like work ethic, intellectual curiosity, ability to work with others]." Notice the key word: STRONG. This gives the professor an easy out if they do not feel they can write you an excellent letter. A lukewarm letter is worse than no letter at all.

Step three is to make it easy for them. Give them your resume, personal statement draft, a list of your experiences and goals, and a brief summary of what you hope they can highlight. Include all deadlines and submission instructions clearly. Send a polite reminder one to two weeks before each deadline.

What Not to Do

Do not ask a professor who barely knows you, even if you got an A in their class. A generic letter that says "Student X earned an A and attended class regularly" does nothing for your application. Do not wait until the last minute to ask. Do not ask via a hasty email with no context. And do not forget to send a thank-you note afterward, regardless of the outcome. These people are doing you a significant favor.

For PA applicants specifically: You will also need letters from clinical supervisors who can speak to your patient care skills. Build these relationships the same way: be reliable, be engaged, ask good questions, and demonstrate genuine interest in the work.

Building Your School List

Do not apply to 40 schools randomly. Be strategic. Use the MSAR (for MD schools), the Choose DO Explorer (for DO schools), or the PAEA Program Directory (for PA programs) to research each school's mission, average admitted student profile, specific prerequisites, and geographic preferences. Build a balanced list:

"Target" schools are those where your GPA and MCAT/GRE fall within or close to the school's average range, and where your experiences align with their mission. These should make up about half your list.

"Reach" schools are those where your numbers are below average but your experiences, story, or background give you a plausible case. Apply to a few, but be realistic.

"Safety" schools (or "likely" schools) are those where your numbers are above their averages and your profile is a strong fit. Do not skip these. Every year, students with excellent applications get rejected from every school because they only applied to reaches.

For MD schools, a typical application list is 15 to 25 schools. For DO, 5 to 15 schools. For PA, 8 to 15 programs. For graduate school, 5 to 10 programs. These numbers will vary based on your competitiveness and financial situation (application fees add up quickly).

Common Application Mistakes

Here are the errors that derail applicants every year:

Applying too late. Most programs use rolling admissions, which means they review applications as they come in and fill seats progressively. An application submitted in June is reviewed months before one submitted in September. Early is better. Aim to submit your primary application the first week it opens.

Ignoring secondary essays. After your primary application, most MD and DO schools send you secondary (supplemental) applications with school-specific essay prompts. Respond to these within two weeks. Pre-write them if possible by researching common secondary prompts before they arrive.

Cookie-cutter personal statements. If your personal statement could be written by any pre-med student, it is not good enough. Make it unmistakably yours.

Not enough clinical exposure. This is especially deadly for PA applications, where hours are tracked numerically, but it matters for MD and DO too. "I want to be a doctor but I have never spent time in a clinical setting" is a red flag.

Applying to schools that do not fit. Sending your application to a school whose mission, location, and culture do not match your profile is a waste of time and money. Do your research.

Not preparing for interviews. If you get an interview, you are in serious contention. Do not blow it by walking in unprepared. Practice with mock interviews, research the school thoroughly, and be ready to discuss your experiences thoughtfully. Know why you want to attend THAT specific school.

Find Your People

One of the most important things you will do on this journey is build a network of mentors and trusted advisors. You cannot (and should not) do this alone. A good mentor will not make decisions for you, but they will listen, share their experience, ask you the right questions, and help you see angles you might have missed.

Here is the thing about mentors: you probably need more than one. No single person has all the knowledge, experience, and perspective you need. Your organic chemistry professor might be brilliant at explaining how to study for the MCAT but may know nothing about PA school admissions. Your uncle who is a physician might give great career advice but may not understand what it is like to be a first-generation college student navigating financial aid. Your older cousin who recently went through the application process might be the best person to talk to about the emotional side of things.

Collect mentors. Each one has different knowledge, talents, and gifts. A professor for academic guidance. A clinician for career reality. A family member or friend for emotional support. A peer who is going through the same process. Build your team.

Who to Look For

A faculty member you trust and who knows you well. This should be someone you have taken a class with or worked with in research, someone who will be honest with you even when the truth is hard to hear. Your university's pre-health advisor if your school has one. An older student or recent graduate who successfully navigated the path you are considering. A family member or family friend in a healthcare or science career, someone with good judgment who genuinely cares about your wellbeing. If you do not have any of these in your life right now, that is OK. Start building those relationships today. Go to office hours. Join a club. Reach out to an alumni mentor program.

What a Good Mentor Does (and Does Not Do)

A good mentor listens more than they talk. They ask you questions that help you think more clearly. They share their own experiences, including their failures and regrets, honestly. They tell you hard truths with kindness. They encourage you but do not sugarcoat reality. They respect your autonomy and never pressure you into a specific path. They follow up and check in on you.

A good mentor does NOT make your decisions for you. They do not dismiss your concerns or feelings. They do not project their own unfulfilled ambitions onto you. And they do not only show up when things are going well.

The Mindset Conversation

"Passion is nothing without action."

Let's be direct. Wanting to be a doctor (or a PA or a scientist) is not enough. You have to be willing to do the work. Not just the fun parts. Not just the inspiring parts. The grinding, unglamorous, sometimes boring parts too. Studying organic chemistry on a Friday night when your friends are going out. Waking up early to volunteer at the hospital before class. Spending months on an MCAT prep schedule. Writing and rewriting your personal statement.

If you find yourself doing these things, even when they are hard, even when you are tired, even when progress feels slow, that tells you something important. It tells you this matters to you. It tells you that your desire is not just a wish but a commitment.

But if you consistently find yourself avoiding the work, making excuses, blaming circumstances, or looking for shortcuts, then pause. Be honest. That does not make you a bad person. It might mean that this particular path is not the right one for you, and discovering that is a gift, not a failure. Redirect that energy toward something that DOES light you up, something where the hard work feels worth it.

The honest truth: Not everyone who wants to be a doctor should be a doctor. Not because they are not smart enough, but because some people want the identity ("I'm a doctor") more than the reality (the years of training, the emotional toll, the lifestyle). There is no shame in recognizing that. The shame would be in spending a decade and $300,000 chasing something that does not actually make you happy.

It Is OK.

It is OK to change your mind. It is OK to take a growth year (or two). It is OK to start as pre-med and switch to a different path. It is OK to realize your true passion is research, or teaching, or public health, or something entirely outside of healthcare. It is OK to not have it all figured out at 20. Most people do not. The students who end up happiest are the ones who were honest with themselves along the way.

You do not have to be a doctor to be "successful." You do not have to be a doctor to help people. You do not have to be a doctor to make your family proud. There are countless ways to build a meaningful life and career. Find the one that fits YOU.

Managing the Stress

The pre-health journey is genuinely stressful. The competition is real. The stakes feel enormous. And social media makes it worse, because you are constantly comparing yourself to people who post their acceptances but not their rejections. Here are some things that actually help:

Talk about it. Do not bottle up your anxiety. Talk to your mentors, your friends, your family, a counselor at your university's counseling center. Verbalizing your stress reduces its power over you. You are not being weak by asking for help. You are being smart.

Control what you can control. You cannot control how many seats a medical school has. You cannot control who else is applying. You CAN control how hard you study, how much clinical experience you get, how well you write your personal statement, and how many schools you apply to. Focus there.

Take care of your body. Sleep, exercise, and nutrition are not luxuries. They are the foundation of cognitive performance. A student who sleeps 7 hours, exercises regularly, and eats well will outperform a student who pulls all-nighters and lives on caffeine and fast food, every time, over the long haul.

Keep perspective. This process is a chapter of your life, not the whole book. People get into medical school at 22, at 28, at 35, at 45. There is no single "right" timeline. Your worth as a human being is not determined by your MCAT score or which school you attend. Believe that.

Celebrate small wins. You aced that biochemistry exam? Celebrate. You finished 100 hours of clinical volunteering? Celebrate. You submitted your application? Celebrate. The big goal is important, but the journey is made up of small victories, and acknowledging them keeps you going.

Questions to Ask Yourself

Grab a notebook or open a blank document. Sit somewhere quiet. Answer these questions honestly. There is no grade, no audience, no right answer. Just you and your thoughts.

Self-Reflection for Your Path Forward

01 When I imagine my daily life in 10 years, what does it look like? Am I seeing patients? Am I in a lab? Am I teaching? Am I in an office? Be specific.
02 What part of healthcare or science genuinely excites me? Is it the patient interaction? The problem-solving? The research? The teamwork? Something else?
03 Am I pursuing this path because I want it, or because someone else wants it for me?
04 How do I feel about the length of training? Am I truly willing to commit 7 to 15 years of my life to becoming a practicing physician? Or would I prefer to be working in 2 to 3 years?
05 Am I comfortable with significant student debt, or is a funded path (like a Ph.D. with stipend) more realistic for my financial situation?
06 Have I spent real time in the environments where these professionals work? Have I shadowed a physician, worked alongside a PA, visited a research lab, talked to a graduate student?
07 When things get hard academically, do I push through and find solutions, or do I look for reasons to quit? Be honest.
08 Do I want to work independently (full autonomy over patient decisions), collaboratively (as part of a physician-led team), or primarily in a non-clinical capacity (research, industry, policy)?
09 What does "success" mean to me? Is it a title? A salary? The feeling of helping someone? Intellectual satisfaction? Freedom and flexibility? Something else?
10 Who in my life can I talk to honestly about this decision? Do I have a mentor, advisor, or trusted person who will tell me the truth, not just what I want to hear?
11 If I were not accepted to any program on my first try, would I try again? If the answer is an immediate yes, that tells you something. If you hesitate, that tells you something too.
12 Is there something else I am passionate about that I have not fully explored because I feel "locked in" to the pre-health path? Give yourself permission to think about it.

Frequently Asked Questions

These are the questions students ask most often. Click any question to see the answer.

Is a DO a "real" doctor? ▼
Yes, absolutely. A DO is a fully licensed physician who can do everything an MD can do: diagnose, prescribe, perform surgery, and specialize in any field. DOs and MDs take the same licensing exams (USMLE and COMLEX), enter the same residency match system, and are recognized identically by hospitals, insurance companies, and patients. The only meaningful difference is that DOs receive additional training in osteopathic manipulative treatment (OMT). The idea that DOs are "lesser" doctors is outdated and simply incorrect.
Can I get into medical school with a low GPA? ▼
It depends on how low and what else your application offers. Look at the AAMC table on the "Real Numbers" tab. Students with a 3.0 to 3.19 GPA who score above 510 on the MCAT still have acceptance rates of 34 to 43% at MD schools. DO schools tend to be somewhat more forgiving of lower GPAs if your MCAT is solid and your clinical experience is strong. If your GPA is below 3.0, consider a post-baccalaureate program or a Special Master's Program (SMP) to demonstrate that you can handle rigorous coursework. Also consider DO schools, which may weigh your whole-person profile more heavily. A low GPA does not disqualify you automatically, but it does mean you need to be strategic and realistic about your school list.
Should I take a "growth year" before applying? ▼
For many students, absolutely yes. About 65% of medical school matriculants took at least one year between college and medical school. We prefer the term "growth year" because that is exactly what it is: a year (or two) where you grow. You gain clinical experience, strengthen your MCAT score, conduct research, mature as a person, and enter your program more confident, focused, and competitive. There is nothing "gap" about that. The word "gap" implies something is missing, when in reality you are building something. A growth year is not a sign of weakness. It is often a sign of wisdom and strategy. Use it intentionally.
What is the difference between shadowing and clinical experience? ▼
Shadowing means observing a healthcare professional at work. You watch, you learn, you ask questions, but you do not perform any clinical tasks. This is important for understanding what a career in medicine looks like day to day. Clinical experience (or direct patient care experience) means you are actively involved in patient care: taking vitals, drawing blood, performing CPR, assisting with procedures, transporting patients, or other hands-on activities. For PA school, direct patient care hours are specifically required and tracked. For MD/DO school, both shadowing and clinical experience are expected, but the distinction is less formalized.
How important is research for medical school? ▼
It depends on the type of school you are targeting. For research-intensive schools and MD/Ph.D. programs, research is essentially required. For primary-care-focused schools, it is less critical but still a strong addition. For DO schools, research is valued but not typically required. For PA school, research is a nice extra but far less important than clinical hours. For biomedical graduate school, research is the single most important part of your application. As a general rule: if you enjoy research, do it. If you do not, focus your energy on clinical experiences and community service instead.
Can I switch from pre-med to PA (or vice versa) partway through college? ▼
Yes, and many students do. The prerequisite coursework for MD/DO and PA overlaps significantly (biology, chemistry, anatomy, physiology, biochemistry). The main difference is that PA programs require substantial direct patient care hours, so if you are switching from pre-med to PA, start accumulating those hours immediately. If you are switching from PA to MD/DO, you will need to take the MCAT and may need to add a few courses (like physics or organic chemistry II). Switching is not starting over. It is adjusting direction.
Do I need to major in biology or chemistry? ▼
No. You need to complete the prerequisite courses, but your major can be anything. Data from the AAMC shows that humanities and social science majors have comparable (and sometimes higher) acceptance rates to biological science majors, likely because they tend to have higher GPAs and bring unique perspectives to their applications. Major in whatever you love and can excel in. Just make sure you also complete the required science courses.
How do I pay for medical school? ▼
Most medical students use a combination of federal student loans (Direct Unsubsidized and Grad PLUS loans), scholarships, and personal savings. The average medical school debt for the class of 2023 was approximately $200,000. Some students qualify for service-based loan forgiveness programs like the National Health Service Corps (NHSC) or the Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) program. Military scholarship programs (HPSP) will pay for medical school in exchange for service commitment. Some states offer loan repayment for physicians who practice in underserved areas. Financial aid offices at medical schools can guide you through the specifics.
I want to help people but I am not sure medicine is right for me. What else is there? ▼
There are many professions dedicated to helping people that do not require medical school: nursing (RN, NP), physical therapy, occupational therapy, public health (MPH), genetic counseling, social work, clinical psychology, health administration, biomedical engineering, pharmaceutical science, global health, health policy, medical education, and more. Each of these fields offers meaningful work, good career prospects, and the satisfaction of making a real difference. Do not let a narrow definition of "success" prevent you from exploring paths that might be a better fit for your interests and strengths.
What if I do not get in on my first try? ▼
Many successful physicians were not accepted on their first application cycle. If you are not accepted, ask the schools that rejected you for feedback on your application. Identify your weak areas honestly: was it GPA, MCAT, clinical experience, or the personal statement? Then spend a year strengthening those areas and reapply. A reapplication that shows genuine improvement is viewed very favorably by admissions committees. Persistence, when combined with real self-improvement, is one of the strongest qualities a future physician can demonstrate.
How do I know if I should apply to MD, DO, or both? ▼
If your GPA is above 3.7 and your MCAT is above 510, you are competitive at both MD and DO schools, and many students apply to both to maximize their chances. If your GPA is in the 3.4 to 3.6 range and your MCAT is 500 to 510, DO schools should be a significant part of your strategy, though you can still include MD schools where your profile fits. If your GPA is below 3.4 or your MCAT is below 500, focus heavily on DO schools (and consider whether PA or graduate school might be a better fit). The most important thing is to apply broadly and strategically, not to limit yourself based on prestige.
What are post-baccalaureate and Special Master's Programs (SMP)? ▼
A post-baccalaureate pre-med program is designed for students who need to complete prerequisite courses after earning their bachelor's degree, either because they changed career paths or need to improve their academic record. A Special Master's Program (SMP) is a more intensive option, usually a one-year graduate program where you take medical-school-level courses alongside first-year medical students. SMPs are designed to demonstrate that you can handle the rigor of medical school. Strong performance in an SMP can significantly improve your chances, especially if your undergraduate GPA was weak. Some SMPs have linkage agreements with their affiliated medical school, meaning that strong performance guarantees an interview or admission.

One Last Thing

Wherever this journey takes you, remember that the path itself is valuable. The discipline you build studying for the MCAT, the empathy you develop volunteering with patients, the resilience you gain from setbacks, these qualities serve you regardless of what initials end up after your name. You are not defined by an acceptance letter. You are defined by the kind of person you choose to be every day.

Keep talking. Keep asking questions. Keep showing up. And when you need help, ask for it. A good mentor will understand, and they will want to guide you. They cannot choose for you, but they will listen, they will advise, and they will be in your corner.

"The only person you need to be better than is the person you were yesterday."

You have got this. Now go make your plan.
